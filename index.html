Assessment #3 - Explanation (5-10 Minutes)

DryWear native image ad, avatar choice, and landing page headline rationale.

This was treated as a test selection problem first. The question was: which customer problem is proven in the market, underused by DryWear, and specific enough for a native ad?

The recommendation is to test the nighttime leak angle for The Sleep Protector.

Your overall strategy and why you believe this will convert

The strategy is to take a competitor-validated problem and make the message more specific to DryWear's offer.

The native format works here because the problem needs context: lying down, worrying about pads shifting, waking up to check, and wanting normal underwear that gives backup.

Why the ad and landing page headlines should work together

  • The native ad copy turns a private nighttime worry into a clear problem state.
  • The image text creates the fast hook: leaks feel worse at night.
  • The landing page headline keeps the same promise after the click: "For nights when pads do not stay put."
  • This avoids a mismatch where the ad sells nighttime protection, but the page falls back into generic leakproof underwear claims.

Core belief: this can convert because it starts from review language, a competitor-proven angle, a clear client gap, and a landing page message that continues the same intent after the click.

Explaining your research/showing your findings that helped create the ads and page

The research used three evidence sources: customer reviews, Everdries Meta ads, and DryWear Meta ads. Reviews showed the customer language. Meta ads showed which problems brands were repeatedly paying to sell.

Apify's Meta ad scraper gave a larger sample size, then the refined patterns were manually reviewed before choosing the angle.

Six-step research process

  1. Review mining: collect avatar clues, pain points, objections, and voice-of-customer language from real buyers.
  2. Meta ad scrape: use Apify to pull a larger Everdries and DryWear ad sample.
  3. Avatar matching: connect the ad copy back to the people seen in reviews, so the customer is not made up.
  4. Angle grouping: sort review language into pain, outcome, psychology, socio-cultural pressure, and identity themes.
  5. Gap check: find review-backed angles that Everdries uses heavily and DryWear barely uses.
  6. Creative build: match the strongest angle to the best avatar, then write the native ad and landing page headlines around that same promise.

Discussing the avatar/persona you went after and why you did

The Sleep Protector was chosen because the data pointed to a woman whose biggest leak concern happens at night.

  • She may manage light leaks during the day, but night makes the problem harder to hide and harder to control.
  • She worries about waking up to check pajamas, sheets, smell, or whether someone else noticed.
  • She wants backup without bulky pads, patient feeling, or underwear that makes her feel older than she sees herself.
  • The avatar-level ad data supports this: 791 Everdries ads, 502 active, and 23,664 ad-days versus 14 DryWear ads, 0 active, and 121 ad-days.

This made The Sleep Protector the best fit for the chosen angle because the message can speak to sleep, privacy, and normal underwear without turning the ad into a shame-heavy incontinence ad.
