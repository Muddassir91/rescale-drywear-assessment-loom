Assessment #3 - Explanation (5-10 Minutes)

DryWear research process, chosen avatar, ad brief logic, and landing page headline rationale.

Your overall strategy and why you believe this will convert

The strategy was built around three decisions.

First, choose a market-proven problem before writing the ad. The goal was not to invent a clever angle from scratch. The goal was to find a problem that customers already talk about, competitors already spend against, and DryWear has not fully used yet.

Second, keep the message consistent from research to ad to landing page. If the ad creates interest around nighttime leaks, the landing page headline should continue that same thought. That is why the ad, image text, and landing page headline all stay close to the same promise: sleep with backup, without feeling like you are wearing a bulky medical product.

Third, make the angle specific enough for a real buyer. DryWear is not just selling leakproof underwear. For this test, the product is positioned as normal-feeling nighttime backup for women who want dry sheets, privacy, and less bedtime anxiety.

Bird's-eye view: this is a low-risk test because it starts with a validated customer problem, finds a gap in DryWear's current advertising, and carries the same message through the ad and landing page.

Explaining your research/showing your findings that helped create the ads and page

The research had two jobs. First, understand what customers actually say about the problem. Second, check whether the market is already spending money on that problem.

Customer reviews were used to understand pain points, desired outcomes, objections, and voice-of-customer language. Meta ads were used to understand which angles Everdries and DryWear were actually putting budget behind.

Apify's Meta ad scraper gave a larger sample size for the ad library research. After that, the refined patterns were manually reviewed so the final recommendation was not just based on a scraper output.

Six-step research process

  1. Review mining: collect real customer language around leakage, sleep, pads, comfort, embarrassment, and normalcy.
  2. Avatar mapping: turn the review patterns into realistic buyer groups, not made-up personas.
  3. Meta ad scrape: use Apify to pull a larger sample of Everdries and DryWear ads.
  4. Ad-to-avatar match: check which avatars each brand appears to target repeatedly in its ads.
  5. Angle gap check: compare review-backed angles against Everdries and DryWear ad usage.
  6. Creative direction: choose the best angle, match it to one avatar, then write the ad and landing page headlines around the same promise.

The clearest gap was nighttime leakage. Everdries had 838 ads in this angle, with 505 still active. DryWear had only 14 ads and 0 active. That made it the strongest low-risk option because the market already shows demand, but DryWear is not strongly owning it yet.

Hypothesis after the research

The test hypothesis is that DryWear can capture already-proven nighttime leak demand by framing the product around sleep, privacy, and normal underwear.

This is different from a generic leakproof underwear ad. The point is not just "this absorbs leaks." The sharper idea is: small leaks feel more stressful at night because they threaten sleep, sheets, privacy, and how the customer sees herself.

Discussing the avatar/persona you went after and why you did

The chosen avatar is The Sleep Protector.

She is the woman who may manage light leaks during the day, but feels more vulnerable at night. At night, the problem is not only leakage. It becomes the fear of waking up to check pajamas, sheets, smell, or whether someone else noticed.

  • She wants to sleep without relying only on bulky pads.
  • She wants backup that still feels like normal underwear.
  • She wants the issue to stay private and controlled.
  • She does not want the identity cost of feeling like a patient or an incontinence customer.

The avatar-level ad data also supported the choice: 791 Everdries ads, 502 active, and 23,664 ad-days versus 14 DryWear ads, 0 active, and 121 ad-days.

Why the ad brief and landing page headlines should convert

The native ad format makes sense because this problem needs a little context. A short benefit ad would likely turn into a basic claim about leakproof underwear. A native ad can describe the actual moment: lying down, worrying about pads shifting, waking up to check, and wanting backup that still feels normal.

The landing page headline then keeps the same thought after the click: "For nights when pads do not stay put." That matters because it keeps the ad and page congruent. The customer clicks because of a nighttime problem, and the first page message confirms that she is in the right place.

The final recommendation is to test the nighttime leak angle first with a native image ad for The Sleep Protector.

The reason this should convert is simple: it combines a proven competitor angle, a clear DryWear gap, real customer language, and a landing page message that continues the same promise after the click.
